SPECIES: cat

FEEDING:
- Adult cats should be fed 2 times per day, morning and evening.
- Kittens under 6 months need 3-4 small meals per day.
- Senior cats (10+ years) may benefit from wet food for easier digestion and hydration.
- Always provide fresh water; consider a water fountain as cats prefer running water.
- Avoid feeding cats onions, garlic, grapes, raisins, chocolate, and alcohol — these are toxic.
- Cats are obligate carnivores and need high-protein, meat-based diets.

EXERCISE:
- Indoor cats need 15-30 minutes of active play per day.
- Use interactive toys like feather wands, laser pointers, or toy mice to encourage movement.
- Kittens are very energetic and need multiple play sessions throughout the day.
- Senior cats may be less active but still benefit from gentle daily play.
- Cat trees and climbing structures encourage natural movement and stretching.

GROOMING:
- Short-haired cats generally groom themselves but benefit from weekly brushing.
- Long-haired cats (Persian, Maine Coon) need daily brushing to prevent matting.
- Bathe cats only when necessary — most cats are self-cleaning.
- Trim nails every 2-3 weeks to prevent overgrowth and scratching damage.
- Check ears weekly and clean gently if dirty.
- Dental care: brush teeth 2-3 times per week or use dental treats.

HEALTH:
- Cats should visit the vet at least once a year for a wellness checkup.
- Core vaccinations include rabies, feline distemper, and feline herpesvirus.
- Indoor cats still need flea and tick prevention.
- Watch for signs of illness: hiding, loss of appetite, vomiting, changes in litter box use.
- Cats are experts at hiding pain — subtle behavior changes can signal health issues.
- Spaying or neutering is recommended at 4-6 months.

ENRICHMENT:
- Cats need environmental enrichment to prevent boredom and stress.
- Provide window perches so cats can watch birds and outdoor activity.
- Scratching posts are essential for claw maintenance and stress relief.
- Rotate toys regularly to maintain interest.
- Puzzle feeders can slow eating and provide mental stimulation.
- Some cats enjoy learning simple tricks using clicker training.
